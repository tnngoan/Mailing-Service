3. Component Deep Dive

3.1 Campaign Orchestrator (Edge Function)

When a user schedules a campaign, the orchestrator breaks the recipient list into batches of 50 and enqueues each batch into pgmq. This prevents overwhelming SES rate limits and enables retry logic per batch.

ParameterValueWhy
Batch size50 recipientsSES supports up to 50 recipients per SendBulkEmail call
Batch interval4 seconds50 emails every 4 seconds = 12.5/sec, safely under SES's 14/sec default
Campaign of 20K400 batches × 4s = ~27 minutesCompletes well within the 30-minute target
Retry policy3 retries with exponential backoffHandles transient SES throttles

3.2 Send Worker (Edge Function)

A cron-triggered Edge Function that dequeues batches from pgmq every 4 seconds, personalizes templates (merge tags like {{first_name}}), and calls the SES SendBulkTemplatedEmail API. Each worker invocation processes one batch, records the SES message ID, and updates the campaign progress counter.

3.3 Automation Engine (Edge Function + pg_cron)

Drip sequences are stored as a series of steps in the automation_flows table. A cron job runs every minute to check for contacts that have reached their next step's delay threshold, then enqueues their email into pgmq. Trigger events (signup, purchase, tag added) insert into an automation_events table which the cron job monitors.

3.4 Webhook Receiver (Edge Function)

AWS SNS pushes SES events (delivery, bounce, complaint, open, click) to a Supabase Edge Function endpoint. The function parses the SNS message, extracts the event type, and inserts into the email_events table. Bounced/complained addresses are automatically flagged in the contacts table to prevent future sends.

3.5 Analytics Aggregator (pg_cron)

A nightly cron job aggregates raw events into per-campaign and per-contact summary stats. This powers the dashboard without expensive real-time queries on the events table. Metrics include: open rate, click rate, bounce rate, unsubscribe rate, and deliverability score per campaign.

5. Email Sending Flow

1

Schedule Campaign

User selects template, audience segment, and send time via the dashboard

2

Orchestrate

Edge Function queries contacts matching segment, filters suppressed, splits into 50-recipient batches

3

Enqueue

Each batch is pushed to pgmq with campaign metadata, template data, and merge fields

4

Send Worker

pg_cron triggers every 4s. Worker dequeues batch, personalizes, calls SES SendBulkEmail

5

Deliver

SES authenticates (SPF + DKIM + DMARC), delivers to recipient mail servers

6

Track Events

SES fires events → SNS → Webhook Edge Function → inserts into email_events

7

Auto-Suppress

Bounces and complaints auto-add to suppression list. Hard bounces update contact status.

8

Aggregate

Nightly cron rolls up events into campaign_stats materialized view for dashboard

6. Deliverability & Authentication Setup

This is the single most important section of this design. Without proper authentication and warm-up, even 1,000 emails will land in spam. Here is the exact step-by-step setup:

Step 1: Domain Authentication (Day 1)

RecordWhat to AddPurpose
SPF TXT record on your domain:
v=spf1 include:amazonses.com ~all		 Tells receiving servers that SES is authorized to send on your behalf
DKIM 3 CNAME records provided by SES when you verify your domain (they look like xxxx._domainkey.yourdomain.com) Cryptographic signature proving the email content wasn't tampered with
DMARC TXT record:
v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain.com; pct=100
(Start with p=none, move to p=quarantine after 2 weeks, then p=reject after a month)		 Tells receiving servers what to do with emails that fail SPF/DKIM
Return-Path SES custom MAIL FROM domain (subdomain like mail.yourdomain.com) Ensures SPF alignment with your From domain — critical for DMARC pass

Step 2: Use a Subdomain for Marketing

Never send marketing emails from your main domain Use a subdomain like mail.yourdomain.com or news.yourdomain.com. If your marketing reputation takes a hit, your company's transactional emails (password resets, invoices) remain unaffected. Set up separate SPF, DKIM, and DMARC for this subdomain.

Step 3: SES Configuration Checklist

A/B Testing Engine

The ab_test_config JSONB field on campaigns stores variants (up to 4). The orchestrator splits the audience randomly — e.g., 10% variant A, 10% variant B, 80% held back. After a configurable wait period (default: 4 hours), a cron job evaluates the winning variant by open rate or click rate, then sends the winner to the remaining 80%.

Deliverability Scoring

A per-campaign "Deliverability Score" (0–100) is computed from weighted factors: delivery rate (40%), bounce rate (25%), complaint rate (25%), and authentication pass rate (10%). Campaigns scoring below 70 trigger an alert in the dashboard with specific recommendations (e.g., "Clean your list — 3.2% bounce rate exceeds the 2% threshold").

Smart Send-Time Optimization

After collecting 30+ days of open data, the system computes optimal send hours per timezone bucket (UTC offset) based on historical open rates. The campaign scheduler can auto-distribute sends across these optimal windows instead of blasting all at once.

9. Blockers & Risk Assessment

CRITICAL

SES Sandbox Escape

New AWS accounts start in a sandbox limited to 200 emails/day to verified addresses only. You must request production access and provide a convincing use case. AWS reviews can take 24–72 hours and may be denied if your use case seems spammy. Mitigation: Write a clear, detailed production access request explaining your opt-in list, bounce handling, and complaint processing. Include this system design.

CRITICAL

Domain/IP Warm-Up Period

You CANNOT send 20K emails on day one. ISPs will immediately flag you as spam. You must gradually ramp up over 4–6 weeks (see warm-up plan below). This means your service won't reach full capacity for ~6 weeks after launch. There is no shortcut around this.

CRITICAL

Shared IP Reputation Risk

On SES's shared IPs, your deliverability depends partly on other senders. A bad actor on the same IP range can hurt your inbox placement. Mitigation: Monitor deliverability closely. If issues arise, consider a dedicated IP ($24.95/mo) — but only once you're consistently sending 10K+/day to maintain its reputation.

HIGH

Supabase Edge Function Limits

Free tier: 500K invocations/month, 150s timeout. At 20K emails/day in batches of 50, that's ~12,000 worker invocations/month just for sending, plus webhook events. You may approach the 500K limit with heavy event traffic. Mitigation: Batch webhook processing (process multiple SNS messages per invocation). Upgrade to Supabase Pro ($25/mo) if needed.

HIGH

List Quality

If your email list has old, invalid, or purchased addresses, you'll hit high bounce rates immediately, damaging your sender reputation before it's even established. Mitigation: Run your list through an email verification service (e.g., ZeroBounce, NeverBounce — ~$5 for 10K verifications) before sending a single email.

HIGH

CAN-SPAM / GDPR Compliance

Every marketing email must include: physical mailing address, clear unsubscribe link, honest subject lines, and proper sender identification. GDPR requires explicit consent records for EU recipients. Mitigation: Build unsubscribe handling into the system from day one. Store consent timestamps.

MEDIUM

Development Effort

Building this from scratch requires significant development time: Edge Functions for sending/webhooks, a React dashboard, template editor, and analytics views. Estimated 2–4 weeks for a solo developer. Mitigation: Start with an MVP (campaign send + basic analytics) and iterate.

MEDIUM

Open Tracking Accuracy

Apple Mail Privacy Protection (since iOS 15) pre-fetches tracking pixels, inflating open rates. Gmail may clip emails over 102KB, hiding the tracking pixel. Mitigation: Treat open rates as directional, not absolute. Focus on click rates and engagement scores for decision-making.

Warm-Up Rules

Rule 1: If bounce rate exceeds 3% on any day, STOP and clean your list before continuing.

Rule 2: If spam complaint rate exceeds 0.1%, reduce volume by 50% and investigate content/list quality.

Rule 3: Only send to opt-in contacts during warm-up. Never use purchased lists.

Rule 4: Register for Gmail Postmaster Tools on day one. This gives you real-time visibility into your domain reputation with Gmail.

Supabase free tier $0/month. Includes DB, Edge Functions, cron, queue. 500MB DB limit, 500K function invocations. May outgrow in 6–12 months. When email_events table exceeds 300MB (~3-5M rows). Archive old events or upgrade to Pro ($25/mo).
Build dashboard from scratch Full control, no vendor lock-in, exactly the features you want. Significant dev time (2–4 weeks). Must maintain it yourself. If dev time becomes bottleneck, consider open-source alternatives like Mautic or listmonk.
PostgreSQL for analytics (vs. dedicated analytics DB) Simple stack, no extra cost, good enough for 600K events/month. Analytics queries may slow down as events table grows past millions of rows. If dashboard load times exceed 3s. Add materialized views, partition by month, or move to ClickHouse.
No dedicated email template builder Raw HTML templates with merge tags are simple and flexible. Non-technical users can't easily create emails. No drag-and-drop. If non-developers need to create campaigns. Add an open-source editor like GrapeJS or MJML.

13. Final Verdict

Feasible — with caveats

This system can absolutely work and will save you $200+/month vs. Mailchimp. But the $50/mo target is just barely out of reach at full volume.

The Honest Numbers

ScenarioMonthly CostFeasibility
15K emails/day (450K/mo)~$45/moHits your budget. Still very effective for most use cases.
20K emails/day (600K/mo)~$60/mo$10–15 over budget. Best value for money at this volume.
20K/day + dedicated IP~$85/moBetter deliverability control. Worth it once you're established.
20K/day + Supabase Pro~$85/moMore headroom on DB and functions. Needed if you grow past free tier.

Recommended Path Forward

Phase 1 (Months 1–2): Build the MVP with SES + Supabase free tiers. Use AWS's $200 free credits to cover SES costs during development and warm-up. Your effective cost during this phase: $0/month.

Phase 2 (Months 3–6): Operate at full 20K/day volume. AWS credits may still cover some costs. Realistic cost: $45–60/month.

Phase 3 (6+ months): Evaluate whether you need a dedicated IP ($25/mo) or Supabase Pro ($25/mo) based on actual deliverability metrics and DB usage. Max realistic cost: $85–110/month — still 70% cheaper than Mailchimp.

Bottom Line For 20K marketing emails/day with analytics and automation, this architecture gives you a Mailchimp-class platform at 80% lower cost. The biggest investment isn't money — it's the 2–4 weeks of development time and the 6-week warm-up period before you can send at full volume. If you're willing to invest that time, this is an excellent approach.
